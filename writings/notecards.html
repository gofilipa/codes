1. Malatino, Hil. On Being Trans And Feeling Bad. University of Minnesota Press. 2022.

Malatino's work inserts itself into the conversation about negativity–what do we do with bad feelings, feelings that don't offer any obvious potential for politics? His answer is that we should look into the ways that bad feelings, what he calls side effects are in tension or exceed normative desires that would otherwise keep us stuck desiring the thing that the medical-juridico and social world are working so hard to keep out reach to all but the most privileged trans subjects. How do these tensions or excessive moments against normativity show us other possibilities for trans being and solidarity?

  • "a commitment to staying with a troubling (in the sense of both troublemaking and troublesome) coterie of bad feelings, animated by the hope that such sustained tarrying with trans negativity tells us more about the existential impacts of transantagonism—how it's managed, how it's resisted, the forms that resiliance and resistance take in the face of it—than any simple celebratory or victimological narrative can" (7).

Brings to the surface affects which would place trans folks within normative teleologies, like "fatigue", and then points out how trans experiences exceed these structures, "future fatigue", which creates anticipatory anxiety in the present moment for the future which will never come.

  • "side effects… experiences of transition that exceed what I, building on the work of Raymond Williams, call 'transnormative structures of feeling'…. transition narratives that rely on and thus reproduce hegemonic, intensively stereotypical accounts of what it is to 'feel like' a man or a woman, importing these accounts (rooted, most often, in white bourgeois gendered norms and mores) into trans lifeworlds where they seem to fit strangely, if at all. Nevertheless, each repetition of such a narrative seems to further entrench and intensify a set of racialized, gender-normed limitations that work to determine how transition becomes legible and coherent—indeed, come to shape and delimit what it means to transition at all" (8).
  • The notion of future fatigue is tied to an experience of waiting to "inhabit the body in order to be the gender that you are" (13).

However, these side effects do have a lot in common with normativizing narratives, don't they? I don't know that what trans people experience exceeds cis experience, but that it offers a blueprint or makes explicit these experiences. Future fatigue is like cruel optimism.

  • "Future fatigue" is like "cruel optimism"… "what happens when you're worn out by your attachment to a future vision that is structurally foreclosed" (14).
