1.2.2. flesh

  1. BFS: surface as simultaneously shifting/moving or unlocalizable
    • Spillers: there is a "stunning contradiction" between the reduction and amplification of meaning in the black body, severed from its will.
    • opening multiple registers for reading surface elements: brilliance -> wetness -> pleasure, even amid commodification/violence.
    • fungibility, reduction to "thingness," creates a fugitivity where objects can signify multiply: reduction amplifies meaning.
      • -> similar to allotropic reproduction? a signal is reinvigorated, a copy of the same, and sent up the stack. In the reduction to a reproduction, there is multiplicity.
    • the Daguerrotype: example of technology where the surface is unlocalizable – where the image cannot be mapped.
  2. media arch: digitality as flickering up the stack
    • rather than believing the digital as immaterial, "eyewash" for the senses (Kittler), Media Archaeology looks for where "information lost its body" (Hayles).
    • Kirschenbaum: digital inscription is a form of displacement, containing a forensic materiality where each bit is unique.
    • the stack: flickering signifiers ground what seems like immateriality to physical signals within the stack.
    • the stack, continued: the effects of the screen are being constantly refreshed, so that screen effects emerge as a "manufactured phenomenon", so it appears as "formal materiality".
      • -> ADD this shows that meaning is continually refreshed, re-created at each step. As it moves up the stack there is a re-invigoration and a grounding, a physical process, despite the abstraction. Something material is happening at all levels of abstraction.
    • (moved from below) The gap between one signficatory system and another creates a force or torque, as between software registers.
  3. media arch: digitality as being volatile
    • formal materiality is a symbolic realm only visible in certain contexts, like a watermark on an image file.
    • formal materiality means that digital elements can move more readily the more formal they are, they attain digital volatility.
      • a paradox: the more abstracted, the more removed from the site of inscription, the more digital data can be "touched" and manipulated.
    • There are two movements: torque (force from shifting) and volatility (unmappability).
