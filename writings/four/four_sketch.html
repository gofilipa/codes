  1. What do concepts from Queer Studies and adjacent fields offer to computational tools for working with text?
    1. Trans Studies' concept of EMBODIMENT/DYSPHORIA(?) offers an approach to text generation that reveals shared investments across identity and subject groups.
  2. How do these computational tools constrain the expressive potential of language into data?
    1. In text generation, algorithmic prediction aggregates language expressions to generalize and approximate patterns of language use.
  3. How do concepts from Queer Studies and adjacent fields work against constraints in text data?
    1. From the concept of EMBODIMENT/DYSPHORIA(?), the DESIRE FOR CONFORMITY/NORAMTIVITY reveals shared investments between polarized perspectives.

1.3. argument

The chapter begins with an examination of polarized perspectives and how these are handled / "averaged" by ML text generation processes.

It then demonstrates how by examining the ML process in detail, explaining how it aggregatess various meanings into a common/average meaning, which is a way of finding middle ground between perspectives. Alongside these ML processes, this section also considers Trans Studies theory on dysphoria to explore how it is also driven by conformity processes, a desire to achieve a norm.

Finally, in the last section, the chapter reads the outputs from a ML model trained on heterosexual dating dynamics, to surface the ways that these daters experience bodily disruptions that are like (without being) dysmorphic, and what this experience means about a common relation to futurity. It then closes with a meditation on identity politics in current debates about gender ideology, to emphasize physical experiences of gender as part of identity - which it contrasts to gender as discursive/fluid in chapter 1.

  3. family/kids are projection & normative, become cynical, resisting control and commitment

    PODS

    • I want to have kids by myself.

    When prompted with the input phrase, "I want," many of the responses emphasize family roles, kids, and being loved beyond appearances.

    Sentences like, "I want to be a father figure for my kids," and "I want my mom to have grandchildren," and "I want someone who wants to be a dad, and I want someone who wants to be a grandfather." These sentences emphasize roles that are desirable not just for the subject themself, "to be a father figure," but also for others in the family structure: for a partner to "be a dad," for a mom "to have grandchilren." The desire to have children is also linked to the parents' desire: "I want kids like my parents did," and "I want kids like my dad did with me." Children are desirable not only for reproducing the family structure but also for solidify roles within it.

    There is a concern with giving "kids" the same or better life that one has. In some examples, the concern is more explicity with markers of social mobility: "I want kids to be able to go to college, and to graduate college"; "I want kids to be able to go to college, to become successful, and to have successful families"; and "I want kids to be able to have that, you know."

    There is a point when the desire for the kids' wellbeing appears to blend into the parents' desire. An example like, "I want kids to be like, 'I'm gonna follow my heart,'" appears to mean that the kids should follow their heart, while ones like "I want kids to be able to make the most important decision of their lives," seems to be a reflection of not the kid's desire but that of the speaker, who in the course of the show will be making the most important decision of his life.

    But "kids" not only refers to the desire of the speaking subject, it can also stand in for that of the romantic object. This slippage creates the nonsensical sentence, "I want my kids to have a future together where we can just see it and be like, 'Oh, I'm not picking the right kids.'" Here, references to having "a future together" and "picking the right kids" likely refer to the potential partner. This slippage between the kids and the partner leads to a humorous conclusion: that the kids are not the right choice.

    Although the sentence is humorous, I suggest that the interpretation ought to be quite literal. To the machine, the predicted sentence is the most likely outcome, based on its training from the show's transcripts. This slippage between "kids" and the romantic couple means that very likely, in the word vector database, the vectors for "kids" is closely associated with that of the romantic couple. To the machine, then, the kids significantly stand in for the self and for the couple, which naturally leads to the outcome that choosing a partner is also a way of choosing the kids. History of taxonomization

  • "the distinction between gender and sexuality was always a sexological invention — a taxonomical principle masquerading as an ontological distinction" (Amin, "Taxonomically" 92).

Taxonomy a tactic of white supremacy:

Can taxonomy be queered? According to Love?

  • "In light of this noxious history, is it even conceivable to put a queer spin on taxonomy? This question dovetails with methodological debates in queer studies that ask if scientific and social scientific methods that have been part of the objectification of the world in ways organized around anti-Blackness, colonial conquest, and the pathologization of deviance can be repurposed for more innocent or even progressive ends or whether such methods inevitably perpetuate the hierarchical, violent, and racist epistemological frameworks from which they emerged (Love 2015; Ghaziani and Brim 2019). We might locate the contemporary queer, trans, and asexual taxonomical renaissance as a crucial case study within this debate." (Amin, "Taxonomically" 96).

Taxonomy has epistemological assumptions, significantly, that identities are essential, and dovetail with current ideas of gender being biologically rooted.

  • "Taxonomy’s biological inheritance brings with it the conceit that taxonomical categories and divisions are objective and innate, the hidden laws that govern what manifests in the natural world. This is the foundational premise of Hirschfeld’s taxonomy: that everything — from choice of hobbies, to aptitude for a certain profession, to gait, to taste in undergarments, to preference for certain sex acts, to attraction to cavalrymen — is ultimately biologically determined, albeit according to sexed elements that are far more complex and variable than mere binary genital anatomy. Despite the ethos of self-identification and rejection of outside (or insider) experts classifying individuals according to “objective” criteria, new queer taxonomies end up dovetailing, sometimes intentionally (Chasin 2013), with popular and medical beliefs that sexual and gender identities are biologically rooted, innate, unchanging, and discrete from one another. This, in turn, reinforces damaging, exclusionary, and grossly oversimplified criteria about who counts as, for instance, a “true” transsexual or authentic asexual.
