Counter-Code: Queer Revisions of Language Technologies

by Filipa Calado

 

This book revisions computational processes that work with language. It applies concepts from Queer Studies and related fields to interrogate computational processes that flatten the complexity of language data. It shows how certain computational tasks, like language modeling, for example, constrain the expressive potential of text forms, stripping them of idiosyncrasies and ambiguities, to transform them into computable units of data. The book then turns to fields like Queer Studies, Black Feminist Studies, and Trans Studies, which inspire methodologies for working against these constraints—methodologies that destabilize authoritative and delimiting structures to open novel readings of language data. Counter-Code works these methodologies into new computational practices that it tests on samples of language from a range of sources, including canonical works of Queer literature, electronic literature, and reality TV. By engaging closely with computational processes, and learning how they work under the hood, readers will discover tools for resisting the ways that technology constrains and determines language meaning.

Each chapter of this book takes up a different computational process for working with text. For each computational process, the chapter identifies a constraint—a mechanism that transforms and reduces an element of language into computable data. The earlier chapters focus on foundational processes for working with text, like capturing, counting, and tagging language data, while the later chapters move toward the more advanced processes of visual display and algorithmic prediction. Chapter one, “Text Capture,” uses web scraping protocols to analyze Gloria Anzaldúa’s Borderlands/La Frontera: The New Mestiza (1987). Chapter two, “Text Analysis,” uses text analysis to study Virginia Woolf's Orlando: A Biography (1928). Chapter three, “Text Preservation,” turns to text preservation methods on Oscar Wilde's novel, The Picture of Dorian Gray (1890). The fourth chapter “Text Display,” examines text display in electronic formats, in a work of electronic literature, skinonskinonskin (1999), by the net artist, Entropy8Zuper!. The fifth and final chapter, “Text Generation,” experiments with algorithmic prediction in a reality TV dating show, Love is Blind (2020). As each of these chapters show, language tools effectively flatten some aspect of language’s expressivity in order to prepare it for computational processing and analysis. By drawing attention to the individual mechanisms that drive these tools, and showing how they might be re-imagined to bring expressivity back into language, readers will gain a practical and empowering skillset for computing over the course of the book.

As the book moves from more preliminary to advanced computational processes, from gathering text to generating text, it also expands its theoretical purview beyond Queer Studies. Drawing from concepts associated with Queer Studies’ theorizing, like borderlands theory, gender performativity, and disidentification (Anzaldúa, Bulter, Muñoz), the chapters in the first half of the book propose strategies for opposing the logics of totalizing structures. While Queer Studies offers powerful methodologies for disrupting existing structures of meaning-making, part of re-defining “queer” for the future involves creating new orientations around being counter, including repurposing, co-opting, and coexisting within components of oppressive systems. As Counter-Code argues, this resistance work involves collaborating across fields to compile a strategic and flexible set of methodological tools. The second half of the book thus turns to fields including Black Feminist Studies and Trans Studies, which are in many ways aligned with, but distinct from, Queer Studies. These fields offer methods that look beyond oppositional forces and relations and toward coexistence and symbiosis—from working against systems to working beside or within systems. Such methods, the book argues, position resistance work as less about subversion and more about creation, of creating space to surface experiences of lived embodiment and shared affinities. The second half of the book thus responds to the theoretical threads spun in the first half, bringing both critique and imagination to the goal of re-visioning technological systems.

Although Counter-Code is situated in a relatively niche subfield of Digital Humanities called Queer DH, it is written primarily for Digital Humanists and for nontechnical readerships from the Humanities, particularly from Queer Studies and Literary Studies. First, Counter-Code’s scaffolded examination of various technical processes and the depth of focus on technological mechanism will appeal to readers from the Digital Humanities who are interested in learning how different theoretical frameworks, like Queer Studies, apply to digital work. Second, the book’s focus on Queer Studies will appeal to readers from that background, who are interested in learning about digital tools within the context of Queer Studies debates and the trajectory of the field. In a similar way, the book will also appeal to readers coming from Literary Studies, who may be nontechnical, but interested in literary analysis and in applying close reading methodologies to digital contexts.

To the best of my knowledge, there are no monographs at the intersection of Digital Humanities, Queer Studies, and Literary Studies. The few works that do exist with a similar focus are essay collections and individual journal articles, which use keywords like “Queer DH,” “Queer Data Studies,” and “Queer Computation” to describe their work. Most relevant is an article by Jacoub Gaboury, called “Critical Unmaking: Toward A Queer Computation”, whose direct focus on using Queer Studies as a lens for problematizing digital methods and phenomena aligns closely with my own intervention. Additionally, a recent edited collected by Patrick Keilty, called Queer Data Studies, offers a variety of “Queer Data” projects in a global context, ranging from historical and archival work on the history of sexuality, to more recent methods in machine learning and contemporary media studies. Due to its form as an edited collection, however, it does not offer the kind of deep, sustained, and progressive attention to digital methodology that is one of Counter-Code’s main interventions. Finally, two other books, Heavy Processing by T.L. Cowan and Jas Rault, and Queer Data by Kevin Guyan, are similarly situated at the intersection of Queer Studies and Digital Studies, although their focuses differ sharply from Counter-Code. Heavy Processing focuses on archival DH projects, and emphasizes theoretical critique, what it calls the “proud lesbian feminist tradition of making a mountain out of a molehill,” in contrast to the hands-on work of building with digital tools which is foregrounded in Counter-Code (50). Coming from the Social Sciences, Queer Data also exhibits a stark methodological difference from Counter-Code and from projects based in the humanities more broadly. Concerned with the practical side of data practices, which looks at data in terms of its utility for documenting queer life, the book departs from mine and the other abovementioned works that look to problematize and productively disrupt data practices and data objects.

The expected length of the total book is 65k words, containing five body chapters, an introduction, and a conclusion. In what follows, I offer a brief summary of each chapter and its contribution to the book as a whole.

Beginning with a close reading of Caitlin Fisher’s hypertext fiction, These Waves of Girls (2001), the book’s introduction proposes an alternative to Digital Humanities scholarship, especially in Literary Studies contexts, which focus on the interpretive possibilities of digital forms. This DH work, the book argues, misses how sex, gender, and sexuality operate as connective tissue between systems of technology and those of power. Rather, DH scholarship might break open and reconfigure computational systems to surface this connective tissue. For example, the hypertext work, These Waves of Girls, which is about an adolescent girl’s sexual coming-of-age, uses hyperlinking as a mechanism that connects the individual pages by affixing links to seductive phrases such as, “I want her,” “school tales,” and “dare” (Fisher). On a technical level, this hyperlinking mechanism evokes the theme of desire within the narrative, which uses intriguing links to tempt the reader through the various episodes of the story. However, a Queer Studies reading might argue that desire constitutes not only the text, but also the network that expands beyond the text. As distributed and exceeding the boundaries of any individual entity, desire thus opens toward operations of compulsion and even automation—operations which I return to in the book’s conclusion, in the context of social media platforms and their automated feeds. The rest of the chapter then introduces the book’s five central computational mechanisms or “constraints,” which include: protocol, iteration, containment, abstraction, and aggregation. Each of these constraints will be taken up in the subsequent chapters in the book.

Chapter One, “Text Capture,” explores the constraint of “protocol” in the context of web scraping, a popular method for gathering text data over the internet. On a technical level, web scraping involves manipulating network protocols to extract text, stored within HTML files, from websites. In order to extract this data, web scraping programs must effectively mimic a web browser and adhere to browser protocols for sending and requesting data over the network. In recent years, however, web scraping has become significantly more difficult due to the hunger for text data by Large Language Model (LLM) developers. As these developers capture virtually all the public data on the web for LLM training purposes, websites are quickly closing down access to their HTML files. This chapter looks at the accumulative logics behind exploitative web scraping practices and considers how one might re-purpose them against privatization. To do so, it draws from proto-Queer, Chicana scholar, Gloria Anzaldúa, who in Borderlands/La Frontera: The New Mestiza (1987) bases her theory of cross-cultural hybridity and communication, called borderlands theory, on the experience of growing up in the Mexico-Texas border. Drawing from the borderlands concepts of land return and the vernacular practice of code-switching, or the toggling between multiple Mexican and Texan dialects of Spanish and English, this chapter re-works the notion of capture toward reclaiming what has been stolen or coopted. It uses Anzaldúa’s theorizations a basis for devising web scraping protocols that circumvent detection to capture text data about migrant and trans policies in the United States. These web scraping protocols re-appropriate what is nominally publicly accessible text data and brings them into a space for critique. As the first chapter of the book, this chapter sets the stage for thinking about language as a kind of tool, as something which can be re-taken and co-opted toward various uses. If extraction and co-option is inevitable, the question becomes not how to prevent it, but how to develop practices of resilience and resistance.

Moving from text capture, Chapter Two, “Text Analysis,” explores the constraint of “iteration” which drives quantitative text analysis practices. It begins with the most basic operation in text analysis, which is compiling word frequencies through counting words and their contexts. In order to obtain accurate word counts, a text must first be “cleaned” or standardized, so that textual details like pronouns, capital letters, and word endings do not skew or misrepresent the word frequencies. These textual elements, which convey subtle meanings and contrasts, are removed one by one in a repetitive process that is known in programming languages as “iteration.” To push against this reductive mechanism, which transforms each word into a standardized, computable format, this chapter turns to Judith Butler's theory of gender peformativity. Similarly to text analysis, gender performativity is based on a cyclical operation, which Butler calls “iterativity,” that repeats a specific series of actions with the effect of dismantling its entrenched meanings. The chapter then takes gender performativity into a new text analysis practice to study gender forms in Virginia Woolf's novel, Orlando: A Biography (1928). Butler's understanding of iterativity introduces randomness into the rigid and repetitive text analysis process, opening the potential for re-signifying gender terms and creating new associations in Woolf’s novel. The chapter closes with some recent critiques of gender performativity and its limitations as a discursive method—a problem that the final chapter addresses from recent perspectives in Trans Studies.

Moving from text analysis to text encoding, Chapter Three, “Text Preservation” examines the constraint of “containment” and how it structures the ways that text data is tagged and preserved in digital formats. It take up the Text Encoding Initiative (TEI), a popular standard, or a set of rules, for tagging, or “encoding,” printed text and manuscripts into electronic formats. The TEI requires that text be sorted into a rigid, hierarchical structure, where inner, “child” elements are bound within their outer, “parent” element. Syntactic and semantic forms of language are thus fixed and disambiguated into discrete elements within an ordered hierarchy. To resist this top-down containment of data, the chapter turns to the strategy of disidentification, theorized by Queer of Color Critique scholar José Esteban Muñoz. Disidentification, which is a method for re-appropriating dominant codes, offers a heuristic for coding difference and alterity within the rigid boundaries of the TEI. The chapter applies this strategy to encode the revisions that Oscar Wilde made to his novel, The Picture of Dorian Gray, prior to its publication in 1890. This manuscript reveals traces of homosexual suppression, where Wilde tempered suggestive and erotic language in the dialogue between the story’s main characters so that the it could pass the censors. Using the TEI to tag Wilde’s revised dialogue allows researchers to delineate irreconcilable interpretations of language choices in his revision without knowing or resolving his ultimate intentions. It offers, in other words, a method that surfaces the contours, but not the content, of what cannot be recovered. The chapter ends with a meditation on Wilde's own position of privilege as an editor of his own text, as an executor of his own censorship. The privileges of his particular identity position exert a subtle layer of control in his text, to show that even the most strategic forms of inclusion are not always enough to challenge systems of power.

Moving from foundational processes like counting and tagging text, the final two chapters of the book turns to more complex processes of text display and generation. Chapter Four, “Text Display,” takes up the constraint of “abstraction” within the computing stack, that is, the layers of software that transport data from its storage in the hard drive to the screen display. In order for the stack to operate efficiently, coding instructions (i.e. the program code), must be separated from its execution, from when the program is actively running. This environment creates an ontological state of displacement where electronic processing always occurs at a level of remove from where it appears to be. Over each layer of the stack, this sense of displacement accumulates to give the final illusion of objects on the screen that are immaterial and unanchored from their physical manifestation on hard drives and processors. To read materiality back into the stack, the chapter draws from two seemingly unrelated fields, New Media Studies and Black Feminist Studies, both which offer robust methodologies for analyzing the physical and “fleshy” dimensions of symbolic processes. The chapter then takes these theories to analyze two works in parallel, a science fiction novel by Octavia Butler’s novel, Dawn (1987), alongside an early example of electronic fiction, skinonskinonskin, by Aureia Harvey and Michaël Samyn, (1999). These two pieces, one in print and another electronic, both explore the collision of material and immaterial forms, particularly as they relate to the themes of sexual desire and intimacy. Reading these works through one another, the chapter shows how the program code of the electronic piece, skinonskinonskin, displays screen effects that, while apparently disconnected from its underlying computational processing, surface a sense of physical intimacy within the stack.

The fifth and final chapter, “Text Generation,” turns on the constraint of aggregation, which is used within machine learning processes to generate text. This chapter traces the steps behind machine learning processes, specifically the training process for Large Language Models (LLMs), to explore how they transform word meaning into computational form. Similarly to text analysis, LLMs take an individual word’s context to compile word frequencies for that word. Unlike text analysis, however, these word frequencies are used in predictive processes, as part of algorithms that guess what text should be generated, that is, which word would most likely follow another word based on previous patterns of word usage. In order to predict words, LLMs must create an aggregate representation of word meaning. The chapter argues that this process of aggregating word patterns, which aims to “normalize” word meaning across various examples of its usage, lends itself to the study of social norms in language. It then uses LLMs to study these norms in a heterosexual, cisgendered context, from the reality TV show, Love is Blind (2020 – present), which places show participants into a “blind” dating experiment. Training an LLM on the transcripts from the show reveals linguistic and affective patterns around the body which not only characterize the normative investments from the show, which ends in marriage, but also an experience of bodily dissonance due to their physical and visual separation from one another. To read these experiences, the chapter draws from Trans Studies theorizing on the body and the experience of bodily disjunction, which often accompany feelings of dysphoria, in transition narratives. It shows how the participants in Love Is Blind—while firmly anchored to their cisnormative frameworks—experience a temporary form of sensory disjunction. The chapter concludes that this analysis, which based on surfacing shared investments between identity groups, offers groundwork for building solidarity across even polarized points of view.

The conclusion chapter explores what it means to take what are reductive technological tools and appropriate them toward the work of resistance. It begins with a meditation on Judith Butler’s recent comments on “passion” as a tool instrumentalized by political social media accounts, such the Department of Homeland Security account on Instagram, toward racist and transphobic violence (dhsgov). Such content, Butler explains, “release or incite sudden and shameless outbreaks of sadistic exhilaration in those they are meant to thrill and recruit, to recruit to that thrill” (The Scholar and Feminist 2026 Conference). Butler wonders how the notion of “thrill” might “recruit” a counter-movement, one that “mobiliz[es] appeals to passions for freedom, equality, and justice.” Butler’s proposal here echoes a fundamental operation in their landmark theory of performativity—of taking a mechanism that has been used to oppress (i.e. the compulsory performance of gender norms), and twisting it toward other uses. The conclusion explores current initiatives toward this end, drawing from examples of resistance movements from indigenous and marginalized communities in New Zealand, Kenya, and Brazil, where activists and workers are taking the tools of AI industry and using them to fight commercializing and extractivist logics. These movements point the intellectual work in Counter-Code toward an activist register, showing how the tools of coding and thinking might translate to physical action.

As of March 2026, four of the five body chapters have been drafted, with two of these still in need of revision. The first chapter, introduction chapter, and conclusion chapter remain to be drafted.

The author, Filipa Calado, is a self-taught programmer who uses digital tools to study queerness. Trained in English Literature at the Graduate Center, CUNY, she is now an Assistant Professor (tenure-track) at Pratt Institute, School of Information, where she teaches classes on Python programming, the Digital Humanities, and Machine Learning. Her research spans various tools that work with language, like text analysis algorithms, markup languages, and programming languages. Most recently, she experiments with “small” language modeling (SLMs) as a method for close-reading, which she uses to study transphobia in language. In her published work, she aims to reach the widest possible audience, writing across Humanities and Natural Language Processing (NLP) venues. Her essays have appeared in the following collections and journals: Digital Editing & Publishing in the 21st Century; The Open Library of Humanities Journal; The Journal for Interactive Technology and Pedagogy; as well as the conference proceedings for GeBNLP: The 6th Workshop on Gender Bias in Natural Language Processing. Her coding projects, which she authors herself and publishes under open licenses, are available on Github and Huggingface (See the repositories under Calado, Filipa in the Bibliography).
